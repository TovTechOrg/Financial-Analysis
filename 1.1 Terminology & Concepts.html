What are Financial Markets?

Financial markets are where buyers and sellers come together to trade assets such as stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities. These markets play a crucial role in the global economy.

Asset Classes

Financial markets offer a wide array of assets. Let's explore the most common ones.

1. Stocks (Equities)

Definition: Stocks (equities, shares) represent ownership in a company. Buying a stock makes you a part-owner.

Examples: Apple (AAPL), Amazon (AMZN), Microsoft (MSFT)

Characteristics:

  • Potential for Growth (capital appreciation).
  • Dividends (some companies distribute profits).
  • Liquidity (large company stocks are generally easy to trade).
  • Risk (prices can fluctuate, potential for loss).

Types of Stocks:

Common Stock: Entitles owners to vote at shareholder meetings and receive dividends.

Preferred Stock: Usually no voting rights, but receives dividends before common stockholders and has priority in bankruptcy.

Growth Stocks: Earnings growing faster than market average. Rarely pay dividends. Bought for capital appreciation.

Income Stocks: Pay dividends consistently. Bought for income generation.

Value Stocks: Low price-to-earnings (PE) ratio. Bought hoping the price will rebound.

Ticker Symbols and Market Capitalization:

A ticker symbol is a unique code for a publicly-traded company (e.g., AAPL for Apple Inc.).

Market capitalization is the total value of a company's outstanding shares (Stock Price × Total Shares). It categorizes companies (small-cap, mid-cap, large-cap).

2. Bonds (Fixed Income)

Definition: Debt securities issued by governments or corporations to raise capital. You lend money for periodic interest payments and principal return at maturity.

Examples: U.S. Treasury bonds, corporate bonds, municipal bonds.

Characteristics:

  • Fixed Income (typically fixed interest/coupon).
  • Lower Risk (generally less risky than stocks).
  • Diversification benefits.
  • Maturity Date (principal repaid).

Bond Prices and Interest Rates:

A bond's price can fluctuate. When interest rates rise, bond prices generally fall, and vice versa.

3. Commodities

Definition: Raw materials or primary agricultural products that can be bought and sold.

Examples: Gold, oil, wheat, coffee, natural gas.

Characteristics:

  • Physical Goods (tangible assets).
  • Standardization (in quality and quantity).
  • Volatility (prices affected by weather, supply/demand, geopolitics).
  • Inflation Hedge (often considered).

4. Forex (Foreign Exchange)

Definition: The global marketplace where currencies are traded.

Examples: EUR/USD (Euro vs US Dollar), USD/JPY (US Dollar vs Japanese Yen).

Characteristics:

  • Currency Pairs (buy one, sell another).
  • 24/5 Market (operates 24 hours, 5 days a week).
  • High Liquidity (largest financial market).
  • Leverage (can magnify profits and losses).

Types of Forex Markets:

  • Spot Market: Immediate currency trades.
  • Forward Market: Trades for future delivery.
  • Options Market: Right, not obligation, to buy/sell at a future date/price.

Role of Central Banks:

Central banks control money supply and set interest rates, significantly impacting currency values. E.g., If the US Federal Reserve raises interest rates, the USD might become more attractive to foreign investors, leading to an appreciation in its value.

5. Cryptocurrencies

Definition: Digital or virtual currencies using cryptography for security.

Examples: Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH), Litecoin (LTC).

Characteristics:

  • Decentralization (operate independently of central banks).
  • Blockchain Technology (distributed ledger).
  • Volatility (prices can be highly volatile).
  • Growing Adoption.

Risks of Cryptocurrencies:

  • Volatility: Significant price fluctuations.
  • Regulatory Uncertainty: Evolving legal landscape.
  • Security Risks: Exchanges/wallets vulnerable to hacking.

Ways to Earn Cryptocurrency:

Mining: Using computer hardware to validate transactions and earn new crypto.

Staking/Lockups: Holding/locking crypto to support network operations and earn rewards.

Market Participants

Retail Investors

Definition: Individuals investing their own money, typically in smaller amounts.

Roles:

  • Provide capital to markets.
  • Diversify portfolios for personal goals.

Investment Goals:

Saving for retirement, education, house down payment.

Advantages:

  • Flexibility in choices.
  • Can adapt quickly.

Disadvantages:

  • Less market power.
  • May face higher trading costs.

Institutional Investors

Definition: Organizations investing large sums on behalf of others.

Examples: Pension funds, mutual funds, hedge funds, insurance companies.

Roles:

  • Manage large pools of capital.
  • Influence market trends.

Investment Goals:

Generate returns for clients/stakeholders.

Advantages:

  • Lower fees.
  • Greater resources.
  • Access to certain investments.

Disadvantages:

  • More regulatory constraints.
  • Liquidity limitations due to trade size.

Order Types

An order is an instruction to your broker. Different types suit different needs.

Market Orders

Buy/sell immediately at the best available market price.

  • Guaranteed Execution: Almost always filled.
  • Uncertain Price: Exact fill price may vary.
  • When to Use: Prioritize quick execution over specific price. Risk: You'll get your trade filled, but maybe not at the exact price you hoped for.

Limit Orders

Buy/sell at a specific price or better.

  • Price Control: Set max buy price or min sell price.
  • No Guarantee of Execution: Order might not fill if price isn't reached.
  • When to Use: Prioritize specific price, even if it means missing the trade. Risk: You'll only trade at your price or better, but the order might not be filled at all.

Stop-Loss Orders

Buy/sell once price reaches a "stop price"; becomes a market order.

  • Risk Management: Limit potential losses or protect profits.
  • Market Order Conversion: Final execution price may differ from stop price.
  • When to Use: Automatically exit a trade if price moves against you. Risk: Can lead to missed opportunities if a stock price temporarily dips below your stop price before rebounding.

Trading Venues: Exchanges vs. OTC Markets

Exchanges

Definition: Centralized marketplaces for trading securities, commodities, etc.

Examples: New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), Nasdaq, London Stock Exchange (LSE).

Characteristics:

  • Centralized Trading (transparent, regulated).
  • Standardized Contracts.
  • High Liquidity.
  • Listing Requirements for companies.
  • Generally safer for investors.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) Markets

Definition: Decentralized markets where securities are traded directly between two parties.

Examples: OTCQX, OTCQB, Pink Open Market.

Characteristics:

  • Decentralized Trading (network of broker-dealers).
  • Less Transparency (less stringent reporting).
  • Lower Liquidity (often).
  • Higher Risk (less regulation).
  • Access to a wider range of securities (e.g. smaller companies).

Trading in Financial Markets

Trading involves buying and selling assets with the goal of making a profit. Different market participants use different order types to trade various asset classes on exchanges or OTC markets.

  • Retail investors might use market orders to buy stocks of well-known companies on an exchange like the NYSE.
  • Institutional investors might use limit orders to buy large blocks of bonds in the OTC market.
  • Traders might use stop-loss orders to manage risk when trading volatile commodities or currencies.

The choice of asset class, order type, and trading venue depends on the investor's goals, risk tolerance, and trading strategy.

Key Insight: Interconnectedness of Asset Classes

It's important to remember that different asset classes are interconnected. Changes in one asset class can affect others. For example, rising interest rates might make bonds more attractive compared to stocks, as investors seek higher yields with lower risk.
